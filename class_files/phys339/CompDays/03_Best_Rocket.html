The Problem

In today’s case, we are going to consider launching a rocket from the surface of the Earth, where we must contend with both the gravity of the Earth and the air in the atmosphere. Suppose you are launching a rocket which is essentially a cylinder with a radius of 10 cm and length of 1 meter. (We are talking hobby style rockets here.) Air has a density of 1.29 kg/m3^33 and the liquid fuel source you are using has a density of 1140 kg/m3^33. The shell of your rocket has a surface mass density of 2 kg/m2^22 and the rocket engine at the base of the rocket has a mass of 10 kg. A long cylinder has a drag coefficient of 0.82 (CD=0.82C_D = 0.82CD​=0.82) and your rocket engine outputs exhaust at 300 m/s relative to the rocket.

Part A

Assuming you launch the rocket purely vertically, so that it moves radially away from the center of the Earth, determine an expression for the net force on your rocket.

Part B

Because of the extra velocity terms in your equation you probably need to solve this computationally. You could do this with odeint, but you have some more flexibility if you use the Euler-Cromer method and I found it easier in this case. (It can be trickier in odeint to specify such things as: “When I run out of fuel, stop applying this thrust”. In Euler-Cromer, you can just include an if statement in your loop.) Assuming your rocket cylinder body starts full of fuel and consumes the fuel at a rate of 10 kg/s, plot the height of the rocket as a function of time. For comparison, also plot the exact result we got for a rocket with no external forces acting upon it.

Optional Tips:

When you are using Euler’s Method to solve your systems, you always need to append the latest results to a list so that you have the entire sequence at the end of the loop. You can do this with normal lists, but you may enjoy using a Pandas dataframe. You can import pandas using import pandas as pd. To initialize a dataframe with named columns, you can do:

df = pd.DataFrame(columns=['time', 'ypos', 'vel', 'mass'])

where each term in quotes in the name of the column you want. Then, at the end of your Euler Method loop you can append on the latest value of each frame using:

df = df.append({'time':t, 'ypos':y, 'vel':v, 'mass':m}, ignore_index=True)

assuming that the variables are named t,y,v and m in your loop. The ignore_index is important! Pandas by default keeps its own index list and will not append correctly if you don’t ignore it.

What are the benefits of a pandas dataframe over normal lists or numpy 2D arrays? First off, if you just display the dataframe via df, you’ll get a really nice columated table in the Jupyter notebook which is much nicer to look at than the normal numpy 2D arrays. Secondly, it becomes more intuitive to access various columns of your dataframe. You can access the velocity column for instance by either doing

df.vel
#or
dv['vel']

This can be a lot nicer to work with than always having to remember what index each value is. Finally, you can get stats about each column. For instance

df.mean()

will output a little table with the average of each column in your table. You can do similar things with max or min.

Note: appending to pandas dataframes in this way is a bit slower than normally appending to a list. Usually the convenience outweighs the cost, but in cases where your code is really dragging, it tends to be better to append the rows to lists and then just turn them into a dataframe at the end of the loop.
